# Sprint Retrospective: Sprint 23

## Summary

Sprint 23 delivered all committed work totaling 5 SP: Items 77 and 78. The sprint stayed intentionally narrow and hardening-first: it closed the Deno permission-sensitive `@ghawb/yaml-import` gap for the supported representative entrypoint slice and restored builder-style helper ergonomics through a generic `JobBuilder.apply(...)` hook without reopening the `@ghawb/job-helpers` package-boundary decision.

The shipped increment itself was clean, but the sprint still exposed one process gap during closeout: item PRs and the sprint PR were squash-merged even though recent repository history had been merge-commit based. That did not break the delivered product, but it created avoidable branch-history confusion after closeout and weakened the repository's normal audit trail. The retrospective focus is therefore on preserving the hardening-first product direction while tightening merge and cleanup discipline around sprint closeout.

Evidence provenance: merged Sprint 23 item PRs #96 and #97, sprint closeout PR #98, the recorded Sprint 23 review note, the current `main` snapshot after review, and the documented sprint/backlog state in the repository.

- Observed that Sprint 23 kept the right product shape: it finished the active backlog by addressing one bounded compatibility debt item and one narrow ergonomics improvement without introducing speculative follow-up scope.

### Scrum Master — Ren Takahashi

- Role-based viewpoint: Ren represents delivery flow, branch/PR discipline, impediment management, and sprint-closeout process quality.
- Observed that the sprint again respected backlog order and single-item-at-a-time execution, and that role boundaries were used credibly: Product Owner and Scrum Master support stayed in planning, review, and coordination rather than implementation.
- Observed that item PR review evidence and Product Owner acceptance were captured cleanly on the item PRs before sprint closeout, which kept the sprint review straightforward.
- Observed one preventable closeout-process gap: the sprint used squash merges even though the repository's recent history had been merge-commit based, and that choice contributed directly to the later "why are these branches unmerged?" confusion because local branch tips were no longer ancestors of `main`.
- Wants the closeout protocol to require either matching the repository's current merge strategy or recording an explicit decision before merge, alongside the existing branch-cleanup discipline.

## Team Improvements

1. **Add an explicit merge-strategy check to sprint closeout**: When merging item PRs or the sprint PR, follow the repository's current history style by default. If a different merge method is intentionally used, record that decision explicitly before merge so branch ancestry and later cleanup behavior remain understandable.

2. **Keep treating branch cleanup as closeout work, not optional cleanup**: Sprint 23 reinforced the standing Sprint 20 branch-cleanup follow-up. Local leftover branches became more confusing because squash merge severed ancestry; the protocol should continue to require deleting merged item and sprint branches unless they intentionally hold follow-up work.

3. **Preserve the role-aware single-item flow that worked in Sprint 23**: The sprint again benefited from strict backlog order, small delegated support work, and item-level review evidence before closeout. That execution pattern should remain the default for small, tightly coupled sprint slices.

## Product Improvements

1. **Start the next planning cycle from discovery or new backlog intake**: Sprint 23 exhausted the active backlog. The product process now needs fresh intake or discovery work rather than stretching Sprint 23 items into broader follow-up claims.

2. **Keep future helper ergonomics additive and package-safe**: Sprint 23 validated `JobBuilder.apply(...)` as the right extension point. Future ergonomics work should continue building on generic helper composition and explicit opt-in packages rather than widening `@ghawb/sdk` with helper-specific methods.
